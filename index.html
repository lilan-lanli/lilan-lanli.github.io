Assembo


Making remote teamwork easy and fun



Assembo is a gamified task management software startup we founded in October, 2020, aiming to synchronize information within project/company efficiently in the remote-working scenario.


Parts of the user interview documentation
Pictures of in-person and online interviews



I experienced many repetitive communications explaining the same project details, and redundant weekly meetings where nobody is paying 100% attention in several jobs. I interviewed 15 office workers working for companies from 5 people to 60,000 people sizes, and they all have similar communication issues at work, which distilled down to information lost within a project, or within a company.

So we are building Assembo, a software to synchronize information/task updates easily, to supplement or even eliminate repetitive communications using IM App messaging, emails, or meetings.



First version of MVP: A spreadsheet of the logic behind the App, with us instant-messeging our early users as the "notification"



After our market research of competitors and target audience, we will be targeting small companies with less than 20 people in the beginning, where this information synchronization problems just surfaced. We found out that more than 2/3 of the interviewees have the habit of checking work emails and messages on their phones, so we are building a Progressive Web App that adapts to both phones and computers, while prioritizing phone adaption.



Second version of MVP: A functional GlideApp with a google sheet as the backend



Currently Assembo has 3 features:
1. Extract task updates and to-do tasks from emails and IM apps, to generate “updates” (If you do not want to share your personal work data, you can still update manually) – to painlessly document your updates within a project
2. View all teammates’ “updates” within a few swipes like scrolling on social media App – to efficiently get all teammates on the same page
3. See all teammates goals(OKRs) on a map – to visualize all teammates’ goals and the projects’ and companies’ direction

I am designing and iterating the UI using Figma with 5 early users, and coding the web App in html+css+javascript in parallel.



Current version of MVP: A Progressive Web App (still iterating on the UI with 5 early users)

DJI


Designing the world's best-selling drones and gimbals



At DJI I worked as a product engineer for 2 years (2018-2020), in charge of the user experience side of the product. I worked on the DJI OM Series phone gimbals for 1.5 year and the Mavic 2 Enterprise drone for 0.5 year.



Mapping out user journey



DJI OM series phone gimbals help amateur and professional phone videographers create high quality video content. I was honored to ride on the first wave of user-centered revolution in DJI (it was more R&D-centered before), and to lead the first in-depth 2-month-long user research on DJI OM3. After interviewing more than 40 users, and surveying 3000 users globally, we analyzed and the most important pain points from the last generation, as well as map out the chain of user behavior for the first time.



Chain of user behavior



In the past, DJI only conducts quantitative research data, and it was focused mostly on the product’s flaws to improve the gimbal, and users’ online behavior to improve the advertising strategy (blue parts in the graph above). This time, I led the first qualitative research and discovered lots of pain points before and after buying and using the product, and specific needs in different user scenarios (green parts in the graph above).



Profiling user persona with our product team



We were focusing on 3 main parts of the entire chain of user behavior: 1 product itself – easiness to use; 2 shopping experience upgrade – showcase the key selling point in an eye-catching way; 3 unboxing experience upgrade. These aspects affect user experiences more greatly than we thought.



Parts of the users' persona – Explorers and young parents



1 Product’s easiness to use:
For example, after studying the behavior of 25% of our existing amateur gimbal users – young parents, we developed features according to their persona – such as low angle shooting mode adapted to kids’ height, and enhanced face-tracking algorithm trained with more kids’ facial databases.



Product design changes from OM3 to OM4 – more friendly to new users



We also redesigned the gimbal phone clamp, reducing the time to attach and level the phone, from 20 seconds down to 3 seconds. This magnetic sticker design enables parents to quickly capture the precious moments with kids, instead of missing out when attaching the phone. The following round of user survey showed that our product's net promoter score improved by 20%.



Iterating on ergonomic handle design, coloring, and surface texture for sense of quality



2 Shopping/display experience upgrade:
In theory, everyone who is using a smart phone and likes taking pictures and videos is our potential customer, so we hope to attract more potential customers (anyone who has a phone) who are randomly strolling in a shopping mall, by showcasing its most key selling point in an eye-catching way. In Australia, the spinning base helped the same store improve their sales volume by 15% in 2 months.

New users are often confused in the beginning, so I drafted a quick start guide with no words, for first-time users to get started in under a minute. We also added our slogan “Create Magnetic Moments” inside of the box, to upgrade the sense of quality.

xPower


Building a power bank charges with your body heat



xPower is a wearable power bank that charges itself when you are wearing it – as long as there is a temperature difference between your skin and the environment. This idea started from a casual conversation with my friend back in 2019 – human body produces lots of energy that goes to waste, like mechanical energy and heat, we were wondering if there are ways to harvest mechanical or thermal energy from human body.


The block diagram of how the power bank works



I researched and discovered a magical part – thermoelectric plate, which people mostly use to dissipate heat for fridges. However, if you use it in reverse, whenever there is a temperature difference between your skin and the environment, the plate induces current.



The magical thermoelectric plate



Now that we have the heat to technology ready, what would be the most suitable user scenario to apply this technology? The bigger the temperature difference is, the faster it charges. So we thought athletes with high skin temperature for long periods of time would be the best first group of users. We interviewed a few marathon runners, and they do have needs for a power bank for their phones when they are constantly running outside.

Hence, we need to design a wearable device that does not hinder running. And that’s why I designed it as a wristband.



Testing the circuit with a thermoelectric plate and a heat sink



I taught myself some basic CAD drawing and bought a cheap 3d printer to start manufacturing. With the help of my mechanical engineer boyfriend, I was able to print the case he drew from my sketch after multiple print fails.



CAD of the power bank
3D print fails



We planned to finish this prototype and start a campaign on kickstarter, but we ended this project due to technical difficulties – the existing thermoelectric plates’ efficiency is not high enough to charge the power bank fast enough. It takes almost 3 weeks to charge a 1,000 mAh battery. And to improve its efficiency, we would need fundamental level physics research where we currently don’t have the time and money for. I would try to continue this once given the time.

On a grand level, this technology could be a new source of clean energy and be applied to more scenarios, and help people in electricity deficient regions.



The 3D printed prototype that encloses all the electrical parts

Automatic Blackboard Eraser


Building a helping hand for all classrooms



Automatic blackboard eraser helps teachers and professors erase the blackboard precisely and quickly without raising chalk dust.


Block diagram of how the Automatic blackboard eraser works



For our senior design project in 2017, our goal was to provide all classrooms an affordable and clean solution to replace blackboards and projectors, but considering the cost of the new installation of a screen and most of the schools are still using blackboards, we decided to design a relatively cheap and feasible solution that can be used in most classrooms across the world.



The friction points and the torque of the motor



We interviewed our professors and prioritized some user needs:
1. Erase the board quickly
2. Erase the board without raising dust
3. Can erase partially of the blackboard



Motor control circuits



To do so, we built an aluminum frame than can be installed on most blackboards, with the help of our campus mechanical shop. And we would need a solenoid actuator to give the right amount of pressure on the blackboard eraser to erase quickly without raising dust. And I designed the controller and power circuits and the frame and rails and the belt mechanism.



The solenoid actuator to press the blackboard eraser towards the board



We also added a small camera module in front of the desk to monitor the blackboard. And if the user wants to erase parts of the blackboard, he or she can simply circle the part that needs to be erased with a red chalk.



How the Raspberry Pi and the camera works

Department of Parental Services


Directing and writing stories on controversial issues





This short film discusses whether should there be a bar exam for being parents, as being a parent is one of the most important jobs in the world yet there is no qualification needed doing it.


Snippet of Department of Parental Services’ script



Most of our “annoying” and “selfish” behaviors can be rooted back to specific traumatic experiences from our upbringing. Does that mean having a better “quality control” for parents’ abilities would solve a lot of people’s behavioral problems from the root? However, is having “quality control” for parents against people’s free will and their natural rights?



Moodboard for the film – high-tech, depressed, and tense



Filmmaking is one of the best methods to raise questions and express thoughts. I was able to transfer some of the skills in filmmaking to problem-solving, such as moodboarding. A lot of the times users are buying the “emotions” the products and services made them feel, instead of buying the great specs of the products. What do I want my viewers (or users) to feel? This is the question I ask myself when writing scripts or designing products/services.



Storyboard of the opening scene



So I wrote and directed a short film that talks to the problem, as well as partially editing and add special effects. A young couple who are not ready to have kids are debating if they should abort their baby, and the Department of Parental Services are going to test if they are capable and willing to raise kids in unexpected ways.



Editing in Premiere and adding special effects in After Effects



To further render the suspicious and depressed mood of the film, I also composed songs accompanying the scene. The theme song is listed below.



Music scoring in Logic Pro X
